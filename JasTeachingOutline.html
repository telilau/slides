James and the New Perspective

Recap of covenantal nomism as presented by Sanders
  • How you "get in" is by grace (God's election of Israel as his covenant people)
  • How you "stay in" is by obedience/works (obedience to the law is the condition for remaining in God's covenant, not earning it)
Jas 2 and covenantal nomism
  • Jas 2 appears to cohere with covenantal nomism
    • Christian life begins with grace (1:18, "he chose to give us birth through the word of truth")
    • Yet, James insist that final salvation, deliverance in the judgment of God (i.e. justification in Jas 2) takes into account one's works.
    • Consequently, we have the pattern of covenantal nomism reinterpreted in the new covenant
      • One gets into relationship with God by faith in Christ
      • But, one stays in that relationship through obedience to the royal law.
      • In essence, we have a synergistic understanding of James' soteriology.
  • This interpretation is possible if we read James alone by itself. But when James is placed within the NT cannon, it conflicts with other NT texts, esp. Paul.
Necessary corrections to covenantal nomism (ignore for now)
  • Variegated Judaism
    • To claim that 2nd temple Judaism demonstrates the pattern of covenantal nomism fails to recognize its diversity. I affirm that many Jews, perhaps the majority of Jews, were more legalistic than the picture presented by Sanders.
  • "Getting in" and "staying in" cannot be directly applied to the issue of salvation
    • While "getting in" and "staying in" may be valid for covenantal status, it doesn't directly apply to salvation
      • "Getting in" as applied to salvation:
        • While "getting in" is of grace indeed, it also requires a definite commitment, repentance, and faith, on the part of the beneficiaries
      • "Staying in" as applied to salvation:
        • If those who "stay in" are to show works, their perseverance is the fruit of grace, their willing and doing is God's doing ("for it is God who works in you to will and to act in order to fulfill his good purpose"; Phil 2:13).
      • We should remember that belonging to the covenant (in the sense of being born a Jew) was not a guarantee of salvation. Jews viewed salvation as a final decision made by God at judgment.
    • Synergism
      • God's grace was necessary for salvation, but the individual Jew still had to obey the law in order to be saved.
      • In other words, Second Temple Jewish soteriology was synergistic. Humans, through obedience to the law, cooperate with God's grace in order to attain salvation.

Resolution of the issue on soteriology: synergistic or monergistic
Jas 2:24 A person is justified by works and not by faith alone
Rom 3:28 A person is justified by faith and not by works of the law
  • Options
    • Paul has the same kind of synergism that is apparent in James
    • *Paul and James have a monergistic view of salvation
  • Interpreting Jas 2:24 and Rom 3:28 together
    • works (James) | works of the law (Paul)
    • faith alone (James) | faith (Paul)
    • justify (James) | justify (Paul)
  • Resolution
    • works (James) | works of the law (Paul).
      • works (James) = anything done in service to God
      • works of the law (Paul) = ceremonial practices of the Jewish faith; those works concerned with separating the pure from the impure (as seen in 4QMMT) | *works in a Jewish context, i.e. subset of the larger category of works (compare Rom 3:28 with Rom 4:1–8). In essence, Rom 3:28 excludes works of any kind from justification.
      • Conclusion: works (James) is fundamentally similar to works of the law (Paul)
    • *faith alone (James) | faith (Paul)
      • The use of faith alone shows clearly that James is referring to the bogus faith that he has been attacking throughout this paragraph: a faith that someone claims to have (2:14), a faith that is dead (2:17, 26) and useless (2:20).
      • This is not the faith that Paul intends. He teaches that faith is a dynamic, powerful force, through which the believer is intimately united with Christ, his Lord. And since faith is in a Lord, the need for obedience to follow from faith is part of the meaning of the word for Paul. He can therefore speak of “the obedience of faith” (Rom 1:5) and say that it is “faith working through love” that matters in Christ (Gal 5:6). This is the same kind of faith that James is promoting in this paragraph.
    • *justify (James) | justify (Paul)
      • Problem (apparent contradiction): works are a necessary basis or means for justification (James) | works have no place in justification (Paul).
      • Justify (Paul): initial declaration of a sinner’s innocence before God
      • Justify (James): ultimate verdict of innocence pronounced over a person at the last judgment.
      • Conclusion: both use justify differently. But they are complementary. “If a sinner can get into relationship with God only by faith (Paul), the ultimate validation of that relationship takes into account the works that true faith must inevitably produce (James).”
    • Quotes
      • Calvin: Salvation is by faith alone, but by a faith that is not alone
        • He writes, “We, indeed, allow that good works are required for righteousness; we only take away from them the power of conferring righteousness, because they cannot stand before the tribunal of God.”
      • Jeremias
        • Paul speaks of Christian faith (trust in Jesus) and Jewish works (obeying the law so as to justify oneself)
        • James speaks of Jewish faith (pure monotheism) and Christian works (good deeds that flow from salvation).
      • Gench:
        • Paul deals with obstretics, with how new life beings.
        • James deals with peadiatrics and geriartics, with how Christian life grows and matures and ages.
      • Fung:
        • Differentiates between
          • forensic justification by faith: legal declaration made by God at the time one commit’s one life to Christ.
          • probative justification by works: demonstration by a transformed life that such a commitment was genuine.
      • Witherington
        • If we take the vindication in Jas 2:24 as referring to the final verdict of God on one’s deeds and life work, then even Paul can be said to have agreed. He even speaks of a final justification/vindication that is dependent on what believers do in the interim (Gal 5:5–6). This final vindication is in view here. Paul would agree that one cannot be righteous on that last day without having done some good deeds between the new birth and that last day in the spiritual pilgrimage.
Resolution on the use of Gen 15:6 (Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness) to support their seemingly opposite view
  • Paul: Uses Gen 15:6 to show that Abraham, and people generally, are justified by faith and not by works
    • Paul uses the chronological placement of Gen 15:6 as evidence that Abraham's initial declaration of righteousness was attained solely on the basis of faith.
    • Appeals to Gen 15:6 to prove that Abraham's faith was credited to him for righteousness, before he had done works, before he was circumcised.
  • James: Uses Gen 15:6 as a descriptor of Abraham's life as a whole
    • James' use of Gen 15:6 does not focus on the timing, but the fact of the declaration of righteousness. Thus, the concern of Paul (that Abraham was declared righteous before he had done works) is not central to James. Rather, James uses Gen 15:6 as a description of Abraham's life generally. The sequence of Gen 15 followed by Gen 22 then suggests that Abraham's faith (15:6) found its ultimate significance and meaning in Abraham's life of obedience (Gen 22). In other words, Gen 15 finds its fulfillment (ἐπληρώθη ἡ γραφὴ; 2:23) or ultimate significance in Gen 22.

Wisdom through prayer (1:5–8)

1:5 If any of you lacks wisdom (λείπεται σοφίας), you should ask God, who gives generously (ἁπλῶς) to all without finding fault, and it will be given to you. 6 But when you ask, you must believe and not doubt (ἐν πίστει μηδὲν διακρινόμενος), because the one who doubts is like a wave of the sea, blown and tossed by the wind. 7 That person should not expect to receive anything from the Lord. 8 Such a person is double-minded (δίψυχος) and unstable (ἀκατάστατος) in all they do.

  • lacks wisdom (λείπεται σοφίας):
    • wisdom: Means by which the godly discern and carry out the will of God.
      • Generally, it is skill at life, particularly the ability to make sound judgments and speak the right words. The wisdom of James is not skill in life, but the divinely given ability to live in a godly way (see Jas 3) and to endure testing. It is not the wisdom of earthly success; it is the wisdom tied up with the ability to live the life of faith (BECNT).
      • In later OT and Jewish wisdom literature, wisdom is sometimes hypostatized almost as though it were a person (a process begun in Prov 8:22–31). In Sir 24, it is almost identified with God’s Spirit, and at times James seems virtually to speak of wisdom the way that Paul would speak of God’s Spirit (BECNT).
  • who gives generously
    • The fact that wisdom is given does not mean that human activity is not uninvolved. We look to God for our daily bread, but daily bread is also linked to work. As there is a link between working and having bread, there is a link between struggling to understand and obtaining wisdom. But this does not alter the fact that wisdom is a gift (BECNT).
  • you must believe and not doubt
    • Doubt can be misunderstood, because in English parlance it means any uncertainty or questioning. But James does not mean never have a measure of uncertainty regarding whether something is God’s will; rather he is condemning a lack of commitment, a divided loyalty, or an indecision or hesitancy that questions the integrity of God. Thus, the command “to doubt nothing” does not mean that one should believe everything anyone says; it means that one should in no way hold back from commitment or divide one’s loyalties. James tells his readers they must wholeheartedly and single-mindely trust his character and promises. (BECNT)
    • Doubt:
      • The doubt that we are to avoid is not uncertainty about the contents of his will. The precise contents are often unknown to us. The true prayer of faith (5:15) is by definition the one that acknowledges “if the Lord wills” (4:15). The doubt we must banish is that which questions whether God is the one to whom we are ultimately loyal, to whom we pledge allegiance.
      • It doesn’t mean that we never question what God gives them; rather, we should not doubt the character of God as one who gives generously. See Acts 2:12; Mark 9:24. (ZECNT)
      • The problem is not the amount of confidence the petitioner has when he utters his prayer. Rather, the person does not have confidence in the nature of God generally. This is contra the “name-it-and-claim-it” heresy.

  • Why did James choose Rahab?
    • There’s a tradition (found in late first-century Christian writing of 1 Clement) in which both Abraham and Rahab are models of faith and hospitality: Rahab welcomed the 3 spies (τοὺς ἀγγέλους) | Abraham also received the 3 men (“angels”). The faith in both Rahab and Abraham may then contrast with the dead faith of the man in 2:15–16 who refuses to give aid to those in need. But why does James not refer to Gen 18 (the 3 visitors to Abraham)? Moo thinks that it is because James wanted variety. In contrast to a famous and celebrated ancestor, Rahab is an obscure Gentile woman of low moral character. Thus, James implied that anyone is capable of acting on his or her faith—whether a patriarch or a prostitute.
    • Difference between Abraham and Rahab
      • Abraham: rich, moral, male, father of the Jewish nation, major figure in society
      • Rahab: poor, immoral, female, outcast of Canaanite nation, minor figure in society
      • Implication: Abraham and Rahab form a merism, so that everyone in between them is included as needing to exhibit transformed living to demonstrate the genuineness of faith.
  • In Heb 11:31, “By faith .. she did not perish with those who were disobedient, because he had received (δεξαμένη) the spies in peace.” Putting Jas 2:25 and Heb 11:31 together, Rahab’s justification is marked by the fact that she is spared.
  • James calls the spies "messengers" (τοὺς ἀγγέλους) while Hebrews calls them "spies" (τοὺς κατασκόπους). Is James’ use indicative that these messengers were the means Rahab discovered more about God and his intentions? (BECNT). BECNT also suggests that there is more evidence how the cord // the Exodus passover.
  • Issue of Rahab’s lying
    • Hierarchical approach to ethics: at times, a more fundamental moral principle must be kept even if it requires disobedience to a more subsidiary one. Here, defending God’s cause was a higher call then responding faithfully to an already doomed regime.
      • Similar to Jesus’ weightier matters of the law (Lau).
James: Faith without deeds is dead (2:26)

2:26 As the body without the spirit is dead (ὥσπερ γὰρ τὸ σῶμα χωρὶς πνεύματος νεκρόν ἐστιν), so faith without deeds is dead (οὕτως καὶ ἡ πίστις χωρὶς ἔργων νεκρά ἐστιν).

  • The Spirit is the life principle of the body. Just as the body ceases to be without the spirit; so also faith ceases to be without works. James is not arguing that works be added to faith; but that one possesses the right kind of faith, a “faith that works.”
